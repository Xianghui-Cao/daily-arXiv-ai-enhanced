### [1] [Two-nucleon systems at $m_π\approx292$ MeV from lattice QCD](https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.16977)
*Kuan Zhang,Kang Yu,Yiqi Geng,Chuan Liu,Liuming Liu,Peng Sun,Jia-Jun Wu,Ruilin Zhu*

Main category: hep-lat

TL;DR: The paper investigates nucleon-nucleon systems in the $^3S_1$ and $^1S_0$ channels using lattice QCD, finding virtual state poles with specific binding energies. It employs Lüscher's method and the NPHF for analysis, both yielding consistent results.


<details>
  <summary>Details</summary>
Motivation: To understand the behavior of nucleon-nucleon systems in the $^3S_1$ (deuteron) and $^1S_0$ (di-neutron) channels at a pion mass of approximately 292 MeV, and to explore the impact of the left-hand cut on scattering amplitudes by comparing two different methods of analysis.

Method: Lattice quantum chromodynamics (QCD) is used with three $N_f = 2+1$ ensembles that have the same pion mass and lattice spacing but differ in spatial volumes. The distillation quark smearing method is applied to enhance precision and ensure symmetric correlators. Scattering amplitudes are extracted using Lüscher's finite-volume method, and an alternative approach, the Non-Perturbative Hamiltonian framework (NPHF), is also utilized to examine the influence of the left-hand cut.

Result: Both the $^3S_1$ and $^1S_0$ channels show virtual state poles, with the deuteron having a binding energy of $6^{+5}_{-3}$ MeV and the di-neutron a binding energy of $11^{+6}_{-5}$ MeV. The results from Lüscher's method and the NPHF are found to be consistent, indicating a reliable extraction of the scattering parameters.

Conclusion: The study provides evidence for the existence of virtual state poles in the nucleon-nucleon system at a higher than physical pion mass, and it demonstrates the consistency between Lüscher's method and the NPHF for the scattering analysis, suggesting robustness in the determination of the binding energies.

Abstract: Nucleon-nucleon systems in the $^3S_1$ and the $^1S_0$ channels are studied in lattice quantum chromodynamics at a pion mass of approximately $m_π\approx292$ MeV, employing three $N_f = 2+1$ ensembles with the same pion mass and lattice spacing $a=0.10530(18)$ fm but different spatial volumes. Finite-volume energies of the nucleon-nucleon systems are determined in both the rest frame and a moving frame. The distillation quark smearing method is applied to improve the precision and to ensure the symmetric correlators by using the same interpolating operators at sink and source. The scattering amplitudes are extracted from the finite-volume spectra using the Lüscher's finite-volume method. At the studied pion mass, both the $^3S_1$ (deuteron) and $^1S_0$(di-neutron) channels exhibit a virtual state pole, with binding energies of $6^{+5}_{-3}$ MeV and $11^{+6}_{-5}$ MeV, respectively. To investigate the effects of the left-hand cut, an alternative method -- the Non-Perturbative Hamiltonian framework (NPHF) -- is used for the scattering analysis and yields consistent results with those from the Lüscher method.

### [2] [Dense $\mathrm{QC_2D_2}$ with uniform matrix product states](https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.17183)
*Kohei Fujikura,Yoshimasa Hidaka*

Main category: hep-lat

TL;DR: The study employs a gauge-invariant variational uniform matrix product state ansatz to investigate cold dense single-flavor SU(2) gauge theory in (1+1) dimensions, revealing characteristics of a Tomonaga-Luttinger liquid and the coexistence of a quark Fermi sea with a baryonic description, which supports the quarkyonic picture.


<details>
  <summary>Details</summary>
Motivation: The motivation is to provide a sign-problem-free, first-principles approach to understanding the behavior of dense QCD, specifically at finite baryon density, using a novel theoretical framework that allows for the analysis of the infrared behavior of the system.

Method: The method involves the use of a gauge-invariant variational uniform matrix product state (uMPS) ansatz, which is a computational technique that facilitates the study of the thermodynamic limit of the (1+1)-dimensional SU(2) gauge theory without encountering the sign problem that often complicates QCD studies at finite chemical potential.

Result: The results indicate that the system exhibits properties of a Tomonaga-Luttinger liquid, with a central charge c=1, and the Luttinger parameter K varying from approximately 1 in the dilute-baryon regime to about 1/2 at higher densities, indicating a quarkyonic crossover. Additionally, there is evidence for the coexistence of a quark Fermi sea alongside a baryonic infrared description, supporting the quarkyonic picture of matter.

Conclusion: The conclusion drawn from the research is that the (1+1)-dimensional SU(2) gauge theory at finite baryon density behaves as a Tomonaga-Luttinger liquid, and the quarkyonic picture of matter, where quarks and baryons coexist, is realized through this first-principles approach.

Abstract: We study cold dense single-flavor $\mathrm{SU}(2)$ gauge theory in $(1+1)$ dimensions in the thermodynamic limit using a gauge-invariant variational uniform matrix product state ansatz. This formulation provides a sign-problem-free, first-principles approach to dense QCD. We show that, at finite baryon density, the infrared behavior is consistent with a Tomonaga--Luttinger liquid: the central charge is determined to be $c=1$, and the two-point function of the baryon-number density exhibits spatial modulation with the wavenumber predicted by Tomonaga--Luttinger liquid theory. The Luttinger parameter varies smoothly from $K\simeq 1$ in the dilute-baryon regime to $K\simeq 1/2$ at higher densities, suggesting a quarkyonic crossover. Furthermore, the quark distribution reveals the coexistence of a quark Fermi sea with a baryonic infrared description, thereby realizing the quarkyonic picture from first principles.

### [3] [Noise scheduling and linear dynamics in diffusion models on Lie groups](https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.17326)
*Javad Komijani*

Main category: hep-lat

TL;DR: The study explores the impact of noise schedule in diffusion processes on Lie groups, particularly for lattice gauge theory, showing a specific schedule leads to linear decay of the Wilson action's expectation value over diffusion time, contrasting with Euclidean models where a drift term must be designed for similar behavior.


<details>
  <summary>Details</summary>
Motivation: The motivation is to understand how the noise schedule affects diffusion processes specifically on Lie groups and its implications for lattice gauge theory, aiming to identify a natural mechanism that results in a desirable linear decay of the Wilson action without the need for an artificial drift term as required in Euclidean diffusion models.

Method: The researchers analyze the diffusion process under a particular noise schedule on Lie groups and compare the observed behavior of the Wilson action's expectation value with that seen in Euclidean diffusion models, which require a designed drift term to achieve a similar effect.

Result: The result is that with a specific noise schedule, there is a linear decay of the expectation value of the Wilson action over diffusion time in the context of Lie groups, whereas in Euclidean models, this behavior is only achieved by introducing a carefully crafted drift term.

Conclusion: The conclusion is that a certain noise schedule in the context of Lie group diffusion processes can lead to a natural and favorable linear decay of the Wilson action, which is advantageous compared to the more contrived approach needed in Euclidean diffusion models.

Abstract: We investigate the role of the noise schedule in diffusion processes on Lie groups, with particular emphasis on applications to lattice gauge theory. We show that a specific noise schedule leads to a linear decay of the expectation value of the Wilson action as a function of diffusion time. We compare this with Euclidean diffusion models, where such behavior requires an explicitly designed drift term, while in the Lie-group setting it arises naturally.
