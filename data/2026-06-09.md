### [1] [Some Inverse Problems in Particle Physics](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.08316)
*Luigi Del Debbio*

Main category: hep-lat

TL;DR: The paper explores the use of Backus-Gilbert, Gaussian Processes, and Neural Network-based fits for solving inverse problems in particle phenomenology, specifically in extracting Parton Distribution Functions (PDFs) from data and spectral functions from lattice QCD computations.


<details>
  <summary>Details</summary>
Motivation: The motivation is to address the central role of inverse problems in current research within particle phenomenology, with a focus on improving the methods for extracting Parton Distribution Functions (PDFs) and spectral functions, which are key to understanding the structure of particles and their interactions.

Method: Three approaches are examined: Backus-Gilbert, which is a classical method for solving inverse problems; Gaussian Processes, which provide a probabilistic framework; and Neural Network parametrizations, which offer a modern, machine learning-based solution. These methods are applied to the extraction of PDFs from experimental, pseudo-, and quasi-PDFs, as well as to the extraction of spectral functions from lattice Euclidean time correlators.

Result: The results of the analysis show the effectiveness and potential advantages of each method in the context of the two specific examples of inverse problems. The comparison likely highlights the strengths and weaknesses of the Backus-Gilbert, Gaussian Processes, and Neural Network-based approaches when applied to the extraction of PDFs and spectral functions.

Conclusion: The conclusion would summarize the findings regarding the application of the three different methods to the inverse problems discussed, possibly suggesting which method may be preferable under certain conditions or for particular types of data, and indicating the implications for future research in particle phenomenology.

Abstract: Inverse problems play a central role in current areas of research in particle phenomenology. In these lectures we focus on two examples, the extraction of Parton Distribution Functions (PDFs) from experimental data (or, equivalently, from pseudo- and quasi-PDFs computed in lattice QCD), and the extraction of spectral functions from lattice Euclidean time correlators. We investigate in detail three different approaches, namely Backus-Gilbert, Gaussian Processes and fits based on Neural Network parametrizations.

### [2] [Certified spectral functions from lattice Monte Carlo data](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.09791)
*Sophie Mutzel,Antoine Tilloy*

Main category: hep-lat

TL;DR: The paper introduces a method to rigorously bound linear functionals of the spectral density in lattice quantum field theory using a hierarchy of semidefinite programs, which is applied to 2D lattice φ^4 theory.


<details>
  <summary>Details</summary>
Motivation: The motivation is to address the ill-posed inverse problem of recovering observables like the spectral density from Euclidean correlation functions obtained via Monte Carlo methods, while maintaining rigorous control over the error.

Method: The authors propose a new approach that seeks to determine the bounds on linear functionals of the spectral density, rather than finding the 'best' spectral density. They formulate this as a convex but infinite-dimensional problem and derive its dual, which is then relaxed into a series of finite semidefinite programs (SDPs) that can be solved with standard solvers.

Result: The method provides rigorous bounds for the linear functionals of the spectral density, even when the relaxation is not tight, and these bounds converge quickly to a regime where the error is dominated by Monte Carlo statistics. The method also has the capability to identify implausible Monte Carlo data through an infeasibility certificate.

Conclusion: The paper concludes that the proposed method offers a robust and reliable way to estimate the spectral density, with strong convergence guarantees, and it can be used to check the consistency of Monte Carlo data.

Abstract: The Monte Carlo method, applied to lattice quantum field theory, gives access to Euclidean correlation functions with well-understood error bars. Recovering the observables one cares about, such as the spectral density, requires solving an ill-posed inverse problem, usually tackled with heuristics that lose rigorous control of the error. Instead of trying to find the ``best'' spectral density $ρ(ω)$, we ask how small or large linear functionals $\int_{\mathbb{R}^+} G(ω) ρ(ω) \mathrm{d} ω$ of it can be, given the Monte Carlo data and the reflection positivity of the lattice action. This is a convex but infinite-dimensional problem. We show how its dual can be rigorously relaxed into a hierarchy of finite semidefinite programs, solvable with standard solvers and enjoying strong convergence guarantees. The resulting bounds are rigorous even when the relaxation is not tight, and converge quickly to the regime where the error is entirely dominated by Monte Carlo statistics. The method also flags implausible Monte Carlo data, for instance underestimated error bars, through an infeasibility certificate. We demonstrate it on lattice $φ^4$ theory in two dimensions.

</details>
