### [1] [Constrained Symplectic Quantization: Disclosing the Deterministic Framework Behind Quantum Mechanics](https://arxiv.org/abs/2603.05072)
*Martina Giachello,Francesco Scardino,Giacomo Gradenigo*

Main category: hep-lat

TL;DR: This paper presents the application of constrained symplectic quantization, a method that allows for the direct sampling of quantum fluctuations in Minkowski spacetime, to the quantum harmonic oscillator on a 1D real-time lattice. The method is shown to accurately reproduce one- and two-point functions, as well as other real-time features, suggesting it can be used to sample real-time quantum observables.


<details>
  <summary>Details</summary>
Motivation: The motivation is to overcome the limitations of the original formulation of symplectic quantization, which had an ill-defined non-interacting limit and lacked a direct correspondence with the Feynman path integral. The authors aim to provide a practical route for sampling real-time quantum observables beyond Euclidean-time importance sampling.

Method: Constrained symplectic quantization, a holomorphic reformulation of fields and action, with constraints imposed on the intrinsic time Hamiltonian flow. This method selects stable deterministic trajectories and defines convergent holomorphic integration cycles. The equivalence with the Feynman path integral is established at the level of the generating functional.

Result: The method was applied to the quantum harmonic oscillator on a 1D real-time lattice, and it was found to agree with numerical and exact results for one- and two-point functions. Real-time features such as oscillatory propagator, discrete energy-gap spectrum, and the evolution of eigenstate probability densities were successfully reconstructed.

Conclusion: The tests provided numerical evidence that constrained symplectic quantization can effectively sample real-time quantum observables, offering a viable alternative to traditional Euclidean-time methods.

Abstract: Symplectic quantization is a functional approach to quantum field theory that allows sampling of quantum fluctuations directly in Minkowski space time by means of a generalized Hamiltonian dynamics in an extra time variable $τ$ which, at large times, samples a microcanonical ensemble. In a previous work we showed that, for an interacting scalar theory in 1+1 dimensions, this framework captures genuine real time features that are inaccessible to Euclidean simulations. That original formulation suffers from two structural limitations, an ill defined non interacting limit and the lack of a direct correspondence between its correlation functions and those generated by the Feynman path integral. To solve these problems we introduced constrained symplectic quantization, a holomorphic reformulation in which fields and action are analytically continued and constraints are imposed on the intrinsic time Hamiltonian flow. The constraints select stable deterministic trajectories and they define convergent holomorphic integration cycles for the corresponding microcanonical measure. In the continuum limit we establish exact equivalence with the Feynman path integral at the level of the generating functional, thus providing a direct link between intrinsic time correlators and real time Green functions. In this contribution, we apply the method to the quantum harmonic oscillator on a real-time 1-dimensional lattice. Testing various observables, we find agreement between numerical and exact results for one- and two-point functions, and we reconstruct characteristic real-time features such as an oscillatory propagator, the discrete energy-gap spectrum, and the evolution of eigenstate probability densities. These tests provide numerical evidence that constrained symplectic quantization can sample real-time quantum observables and offers a practical route beyond Euclidean-time importance sampling.

### [2] [Discretisation effects of gradient flows in QCD-like theories on the lattice](https://arxiv.org/abs/2603.05155)
*Pietro Butti,Michele Della Morte,Benjamin Jäger,Sofie Martins,J. Tobias Tsang*

Main category: hep-lat

TL;DR: The paper discusses large-scale lattice studies of the Corrigan--Ramond large-$N_C$ limit of Yang-Mills theory with a two-index antisymmetric fermion, focusing on topological properties and discretisation effects for $N_C=4,5,6$. The authors find that simulations may be affected by discretisation effects of about 10%.


<details>
  <summary>Details</summary>
Motivation: The motivation is to explore the large-$N_C$ limit of Yang-Mills theory coupled with a two-index antisymmetric fermion through large-scale lattice studies, which could provide insights into SUSY Yang-Mills. The study also aims to understand the topological properties and the impact of discretisation effects on the simulations.

Method: The method involves generating ensembles for different values of $N_C$ (4, 5, 6) at various lattice spacings (0.11 - 0.08 fm). A gluonic definition of the topological charge along with gradient flow is used to analyze the effect of different discretisations of the kernel action. Additionally, ratios of different reference flow times are considered to estimate discretisation effects.

Result: The results show quantitative differences between Wilson and over-improved flows such as DBW2, in terms of their impact on topological charges. The study concludes that current simulations might be affected by discretisation effects of the order of 10%.

Conclusion: The conclusion is that there are significant discretisation effects present in the simulations, which need to be accounted for when interpreting the results. The differences observed between Wilson and over-improved flows suggest that the choice of flow can have an impact on the topological properties of the system.

Abstract: Recent software advances now allow large-scale lattice studies of the Corrigan--Ramond large-$N_C$ limit of Yang-Mills theory coupled with a two-index antisymmetric fermion, providing a path to SUSY Yang-Mills. We are currently generating ensembles for $N_C=4,5,6$ for lattice spacings in the range $0.11 - 0.08$ fm. We report on two aspects of our work: the study of topological properties as well as estimates of discretisation effects. The first aspect is relevant since naively, fractional topological charges might be expected in our simulations. Using a gluonic definition of the topological charge combined with gradient flow, we perform an analysis of the effect of different discretisations of the kernel action, from which we identify and interpret quantitative differences between Wilson and over-improved flows such as DBW2. The second aspect is addressed by considering ratios of different reference flow times. We conclude that our current simulations might be affected by discretisation effects of order 10\%.

</details>


### [3] [Intrinsic Width of the flux tube in 2+1 dimensional Yang-Mills theories](https://arxiv.org/abs/2603.05323)
*Lorenzo Verzichelli,Michele Caselle,Elia Cellini,Alessandro Nada,Dario Panfalone*

Main category: hep-lat

TL;DR: The paper discusses the intrinsic width of the flux tube profile in (2+1)-dimensional Yang-Mills theory with SU(2) gauge group, showing that it remains constant at low temperatures and increases as the deconfinement temperature is approached.


<details>
  <summary>Details</summary>
Motivation: To understand the behavior of the intrinsic width of the flux tube profile across a range of temperatures, particularly how it changes as the system approaches the deconfinement phase transition.

Method: By analyzing the exponentially decaying tails of the flux tube profile, the authors identify the intrinsic width as a characteristic length scale. They then study this width over a broad range of temperatures, ensuring to check its independence from the flux tube's length.

Result: The results indicate that the intrinsic width of the flux tube is constant at lower temperatures but begins to grow as the system approaches the deconfinement temperature, consistent with the Svetitsky-Yaffe mapping and the universality class of the phase transition.

Conclusion: The intrinsic width of the flux tube provides insight into the nature of the confinement-deconfinement phase transition in (2+1)-dimensional Yang-Mills theory, with its behavior being consistent with theoretical predictions.

Abstract: We present our updated results on the intrinsic width of the profile of the flux tube in (2+1)-dimensional Yang-Mills theory with SU(2) gauge group. We identify the intrinsic width as the characteristic length scale of the exponentially decaying tails of the profile of the flux tube. Inspecting a broad range of temperature, we check that this length does not depend on the length of the flux tube. Our estimations of the intrinsic width show a constant value at low temperature and a growing trend approaching the deconfinement temperature that can be understood from the universality class of the phase transition via the Svetitsky-Yaffe mapping.

</details>
