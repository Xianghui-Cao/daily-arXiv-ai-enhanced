### [1] [On the effective restoration of $U(1)_A$ symmetry at finite temperature](https://arxiv.org/abs/2604.11916)
*Gert Aarts,Chris Allton,Ryan Bignell,Benjamin Jäger,Seyong Kim,Jon-Ivar Skullerud,Antonio Smecca*

Main category: hep-lat

TL;DR: The study investigates the restoration of $U(1)_A$ symmetry in QCD at finite temperatures using anisotropic lattice QCD simulations, finding that this symmetry is effectively restored at a temperature of 319(22) MeV, which is significantly above the chiral crossover temperature.


<details>
  <summary>Details</summary>
Motivation: The motivation is to determine the temperature at which the $U(1)_A$ symmetry, which is broken by the axial anomaly in massless QCD, becomes effectively restored. This is important for understanding the chiral transition and the QCD phase diagram.

Method: The researchers used anisotropic lattice QCD ensembles with Wilson-clover fermions, provided by the Fastsum collaboration, to study the degeneracy of flavour non-singlet pseudoscalar and scalar susceptibilities over a wide range of temperatures. The fine temporal resolution of their Generation 3 ensembles allowed for precise determination of the degeneracy temperature.

Result: The result shows evidence for the effective restoration of $U(1)_A$ symmetry at a temperature of 319(22) MeV, which is well above the chiral crossover temperature.

Conclusion: The conclusion is that the effective restoration of $U(1)_A$ symmetry occurs at a higher temperature than the chiral crossover, providing insight into the nature of the QCD phase transition and the structure of the QCD phase diagram.

Abstract: The $U(1)_A$ symmetry of the massless QCD Lagrangian is explicitly broken by the axial anomaly, but it may be effectively restored at finite temperature. Determining the temperature at which this occurs is important for understanding the chiral transition and the structure of the QCD phase diagram. A commonly used probe of effective $U(1)_A$ restoration is the degeneracy of flavour non-singlet pseudoscalar and scalar susceptibilities. Using anisotropic lattice QCD ensembles with Wilson-clover fermions generated by the \textsc{Fastsum} collaboration, we study this degeneracy through hadronic correlation functions over a wide range of temperatures. The fine temporal resolution of our Generation 3 ensembles allows us to determine the temperature at which the pseudoscalar and scalar channels become degenerate. We find evidence for the effective restoration of $U(1)_A$ symmetry at $T_{U(1)_A}=319(22)$ MeV, well above the chiral crossover temperature.

### [2] [High-precision lattice determination of the interaction potential of an SU(2) solitonic dipole and comparison with perturbative QED](https://arxiv.org/abs/2604.12021)
*Manfried Faber,Rudolf Golubich*

Main category: hep-lat

TL;DR: The study uses improved lattice simulations to determine the interaction potential of a solitonic dipole, finding that at large separations it matches the classical Coulomb potential with a small energy shift, and at shorter distances, it shows deviations in line with perturbative Quantum Electrodynamics, accurately reproducing the inverse fine-structure constant.


<details>
  <summary>Details</summary>
Motivation: To understand the interaction potential of a solitonic dipole in the singlet state, especially how it behaves at different separations and whether it can be described by the classical Coulomb potential or if quantum corrections are needed.

Method: Improved lattice simulations were used to model two stationary solitons as an SU(2) field. The interaction potential was derived from the energy of two-soliton configurations across varying distances.

Result: At large separations, the interaction potential aligns with the classical Coulomb potential, with a minor energy shift due to numerical precision limits. At closer distances, the potential deviates, showing consistency with perturbative QED and accurately reflecting the running of the fine-structure constant, with the inverse fine-structure constant reproduced.

Conclusion: The research validates the use of lattice simulations for studying solitonic dipoles and confirms that their interaction potential follows the classical Coulomb potential at long-range, while short-range interactions require quantum electrodynamics for an accurate description.

Abstract: We determine the interaction potential of a solitonic dipole in the singlet state, modeled as an SU(2) field, using improved lattice simulations of two stationary solitons at varying separations. The potential is extracted from the energy of two-soliton configurations as a function of distance. At large separations, the interaction reproduces the classical Coulomb potential quantitatively up to an energy shift $δE_\infty\approx 9\;\text{keV}$ of the fitted asymptotic constant relative to $2m_ec_0^2$, assumed to be related to limited numerical precision on the lattice. At shorter distances, deviations from the Coloumb potential of point-like charges appear, that are in qualitative agreement with the asymptotic formula of perturbative Quantum Electrodynamics, reflecting the running of the fine-structure constant, with the inverse fine-structure constant ($α^{-1} \approx 137$) reproduced.

Result: The results show that the new method effectively reduces residual finite-volume effects and provides a robust framework for determining meson charge radii, even under conditions of small volume and large radius. The method was validated using a monopole form factor for mock data and applied to $N_f=2+1$ gauge ensembles at specific pion masses, demonstrating its practical utility.

Conclusion: The conclusion is that the proposed method, which incorporates an auxiliary function, offers a significant improvement over existing techniques by providing a more precise determination of meson charge radii, with reduced sensitivity to finite-volume effects and without the need to assume a particular form for the form factor.

Abstract: We propose a variant of the model-independent method for determining meson charge radii from spatial moments of correlation functions on the lattice. By contrast, model-independent methods based on spatial moments provide a useful framework for determining the slope of the form factor without assuming its functional form.
