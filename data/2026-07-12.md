### [1] [Diffusion Models for Sampling Near Criticality in Lattice Field Theories](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.08505)
*Yang-yang Tan,Gert Aarts,Diaa E. Habibi,Biagio Lucini,Lingxiao Wang*

Main category: hep-lat

TL;DR: The paper explores the use of generative diffusion models as denoising samplers for 2D and 3D lattice φ⁴ theory, demonstrating their ability to reproduce scalar observables and a momentum-space propagator. It introduces diagnostics and an effective sample size metric, and shows that a model trained on smaller lattices can generalize to larger, unseen lattices, improving critical observables without retraining.

Motivation: The motivation is to evaluate the effectiveness of generative diffusion models in simulating complex physical systems, specifically lattice φ⁴ theory, and to explore if such models can be generalized across different system sizes, which could lead to more efficient sampling methods for large-volume systems.

Method: The researchers used generative diffusion models as reverse-SDE samplers, validated against ensembles generated by Fourier-accelerated HMC combined with Wolff cluster updates. They also introduced two local diagnostics and an HMC-referenced effective sample size (ESS) to assess the learned drift and the quality of the samples. A fully convolutional architecture was employed with weights shared across different volumes to enable cross-volume training and generalization.

Result: The results show that the reverse-SDE sampler successfully reproduces scalar observables and the momentum-space propagator, with some residual bias. The model demonstrated cross-volume generalization, where a 3D model trained on various lattice sizes was able to reproduce the propagator and most scalar observables at an unseen lattice size, with improvements in several critical observables.

Conclusion: The study concludes that generative diffusion models are viable for denoising sampling in lattice field theories, and that cross-volume generalization allows for efficient transfer of the learned score from small to large lattices, providing a promising approach for large-volume sampling without the need for retraining.

Abstract: We investigate generative diffusion models as denoising samplers for two- and three-dimensional lattice $φ^4$ theory across the symmetric, near-critical, and broken phases. Validated against ensembles generated by Fourier-accelerated HMC combined with Wolff cluster updates, the reverse-SDE sampler reproduces scalar observables and the momentum-space propagator $G(|k|)$, with residual bias concentrated in the zero-mode and, in three dimensions, the action density. We introduce two local diagnostics and an HMC-referenced effective sample size (ESS), which probe the learned drift directly, through a Metropolis-adjusted Langevin acceptance rate, and through observable-level bias and variance. Exploiting a fully convolutional architecture with weights shared across different volumes ($V=L^D$), we show that cross-volume training transfers to unseen sizes, matching or slightly improving in-distribution training in the two-dimensional symmetric and broken phases. A three-dimensional model trained on $L \in \{4, 8, 16, 32\}$ reproduces the propagator and most scalar observables at the unseen lattice size $L = 64$ across the phase diagram, with the residual susceptibility excess in the broken phase as the main exception, and improves several critical observables relative to in-distribution $L = 64$ training. This establishes cross-volume generalization as a viable mechanism for large-volume sampling, and the score learned from many cheap small-lattice configurations transfers to the target volume without retraining.
