### [1] [Towards four-pion effects in multi-hadron decays](https://arxiv.org/abs/2603.23609)
*Rajnandini Mukherjee,Maxwell T. Hansen*

Main category: hep-lat

TL;DR: A new formalism is introduced to address the challenges of treating four-particle states in lattice calculations, which relates two- and four-particle finite-volume energies and matrix elements to infinite-volume theory couplings. The method provides a quantization condition that can be used to plot volume-dependent energies, showing clear signatures of two- and four-particle states and their interactions. This has implications for understanding four-particle contributions in decay and transition amplitudes, such as in hadronic D decays.


<details>
  <summary>Details</summary>
Motivation: The motivation is to develop a method to rigorously treat four-particle intermediate and final states within lattice calculations, which are essential for studying scattering, decay amplitudes, and long-distance matrix elements but have been challenging due to the complexity of these systems.

Method: The researchers developed a new formalism that perturbatively connects two- and four-particle finite-volume properties with the couplings from the infinite-volume theory. They considered leading-order effects in various couplings and included finite-volume corrections for two-to-two subprocesses. A quantization condition was derived and numerically implemented to demonstrate the energy levels up to the six-particle threshold.

Result: The result is a set of numerical solutions that display characteristics of two- and four-particle states, including avoided level crossings, which are especially sensitive to the two-to-four coupling. The solutions also provide a basis for identifying the four-particle contributions in processes like hadronic D decays.

Conclusion: The new formalism offers a promising approach to incorporate four-particle dynamics into lattice QCD, allowing for a more accurate description of scattering and decay processes. It provides a way to quantify four-particle contributions, which is critical for advancing our understanding of particle interactions in high-energy physics.

Abstract: The rigorous treatment of four-particle intermediate and final states poses a major challenge for lattice calculations of scattering and decay amplitudes, as well as long-distance matrix elements. As a step towards addressing these challenges, we present a new formalism that perturbatively relates two- and four-particle finite-volume energies and matrix elements to the couplings of the infinite-volume theory. Our method works at leading order in the two-to-two, four-to-four, and two-to-four couplings of the theory, while also capturing the leading finite-volume effects associated with two-to-two subprocess scattering in the four-particle sector. The result takes the form of a quantization condition which we implement numerically to produce a plot of volume-dependent energies for center-of-mass energies up to the six-particle threshold. The solutions exhibit a clear signature of two- and four-particle-like states and the avoided level crossings between them, which are particularly sensitive to the two-to-four coupling. We further discuss the implications of this formalism for quantifying the four-particle contributions in decay and transition amplitudes (e.g. for hadronic $D$ decays).

### [2] [$B \to π$, $B_{(s)} \to D_{(s)}$ from 2+1+1 Flavor Lattice QCD](https://arxiv.org/abs/2603.23702)

Main category: hep-lat

TL;DR: The paper discusses a lattice-QCD calculation of hadronic form factors for B-meson semileptonic decays, using improved quark actions and ensembles with various lattice spacings. It presents preliminary results for scalar and vector form factors, aiming for high-precision determinations of |V_cb| as part of a larger program on weak B-meson decays.

Method: The method involves using highly improved staggered quark action for both valence and sea quarks on MILC's 2+1+1-flavor ensembles with different lattice spacings, including physical pion masses. The study analyzes two- and three-point correlation functions, and it computes form factors at the physical b-quark mass on the finest lattices.

Result: Preliminary results are presented for chiral-continuum fits of the scalar and vector form factors for B_(s) to D_(s) transitions, indicating progress towards the goal of high-precision form factor determinations.

Conclusion: The work contributes to the ongoing efforts by the Fermilab Lattice and MILC Collaborations in the precise study of weak B-meson decays, with the aim of achieving a percent-level accuracy in the form factor determinations.

Abstract: We present a lattice-QCD calculation of the hadronic form factors for $B$-meson semileptonic decays computed using the highly improved staggered quark action for both valence and sea quarks on the MILC collaborations 2+1+1-flavor ensembles with lattice spacing ranging from 0.09 fm to 0.03 fm, many with physical pion masses On our finest ensembles, we compute the form factors directly at the physical $b$-quark mass. We discuss the computational setup and analysis strategies for two- and three-point correlation functions. For $B_{(s)} \to D_{(s)}$ we present preliminary results of chiral-continuum fits for the scalar and vector form factors. The goal of this project is a percent-level determination of the scalar and vector form factors to enable high-precision determinations of $|V_{cb}|$. This work fits into a broader program of lattice-QCD studies of weak $B$-meson decays by the Fermilab Lattice and MILC Collaborations.

### [3] [Thermalization of SU(2) Lattice Gauge Fields on Quantum Computers](https://arxiv.org/abs/2603.23948)
*Jiunn-Wei Chen,Yu-Ting Chen,Ghanashyam Meher,Berndt Müller,Andreas Schäfer,Xiaojun Yao*

Main category: hep-lat

TL;DR: Researchers simulated thermalization dynamics of SU(2) pure gauge theory on linear plaquette chains using IBM quantum computers, showing that even with current noisy quantum computing technology, it's feasible to study local thermalization in chaotic systems like nonabelian lattice gauge theories. The results from the quantum hardware, after error mitigation, matched well with classical simulations for up to 101 plaquettes.


<details>
  <summary>Details</summary>
Motivation: The motivation is to explore the feasibility of simulating thermalization dynamics in a complex system, such as SU(2) pure gauge theory, on current noisy intermediate-scale quantum (NISQ) computers, and to assess how well these simulations can match classical simulation results after error mitigation techniques are applied.

Method: The method involves simulating the thermalization dynamics of minimally truncated SU(2) pure gauge theory on linear plaquette chains of varying lengths using IBM quantum computers. The team also measures the time dependence of the entanglement spectrum, Rényi-2 entropy, and anti-flatness on small subsystems, and applies error mitigation to the quantum hardware results.

Result: The results show that the quantum hardware outputs, after undergoing error mitigation, are in agreement with the extrapolated outcomes from classical simulators for chains made up of up to 101 plaquettes. This suggests that local thermalization studies of chaotic quantum systems, such as nonabelian lattice gauge theories, are viable on present-day noisy quantum computing platforms.

Conclusion: The conclusion is that local thermalization studies for chaotic quantum systems, including nonabelian lattice gauge theories, can be successfully carried out on existing noisy quantum computing platforms, with the quantum results matching well to those obtained from classical simulations once proper error mitigation strategies are implemented.

Abstract: We simulate the thermalization dynamics for minimally truncated SU(2) pure gauge theory on linear plaquette chains with up to 151 plaquettes using IBM quantum computers. We study the time dependence of the entanglement spectrum, Rényi-2 entropy and anti-flatness on small subsystems. The quantum hardware results obtained after error mitigation agree with extrapolated classical simulator results for chains consisting of up to 101 plaquettes. Our results demonstrate the feasibility of local thermalization studies for chaotic quantum systems, such as nonabelian lattice gauge theories, on current noisy quantum computing platforms.

Main category: hep-lat

TL;DR: The paper presents the OS-GHOTRG method for QCD with staggered quarks, which is used to compute thermodynamical observables up to third order in the inverse coupling. The method's applicability near phase transitions is improved by fitting to transition functions.

  <summary>Details</summary>
Motivation: The motivation of the paper is to develop a new method (OS-GHOTRG) that can be used to calculate the strong-coupling expansions of thermodynamical observables in QCD with staggered quarks, and to extend the range of its applicability even near phase transitions where the expansion may not be as accurate.

Method: The authors introduce the order-separated Grassmann higher-order tensor renormalization group (OS-GHOTRG) method and apply it to QCD with staggered quarks in the context of the strong-coupling expansion. They use this method to determine the coefficients of the partition function, which allows them to obtain the expansions of thermodynamical observables such as free energy, particle-number density, and chiral condensate, up to third order in the inverse coupling β.

Result: The results show that the OS-GHOTRG method can be used to compute the strong-coupling expansions of the free energy, particle-number density, and chiral condensate as a function of the chemical potential. Although the strong-coupling expansion is only a good approximation at small β, the range of applicability of the method is extended through the use of fits to specific transition functions.

Conclusion: The conclusion of the paper is that the OS-GHOTRG method is effective for calculating the strong-coupling expansions of thermodynamical observables in QCD with staggered quarks, and that the accuracy and applicability of the method can be significantly enhanced by using fits to judiciously chosen transition functions, particularly near the phase transition region.

Abstract: We introduce the order-separated Grassmann higher-order tensor renormalization group (OS-GHOTRG) method for QCD with staggered quarks in the strong-coupling expansion. The method allows us to determine the expansion coefficients of the partition function, from which we can obtain the strong-coupling expansions of thermodynamical observables. We use the method in two dimensions to compute the free energy, the particle-number density, and the chiral condensate as a function of the chemical potential up to third order in the inverse coupling $β$. Although near the phase transition the expansion is only a good approximation to the full theory at small $β$, we show that the range of applicability can be greatly extended by fits to judiciously chosen transition functions.
