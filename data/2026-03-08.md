### [1] [Constrained Symplectic Quantization: Disclosing the Deterministic Framework Behind Quantum Mechanics](https://arxiv.org/abs/2603.05072)
*Martina Giachello,Francesco Scardino,Giacomo Gradenigo*

Main category: hep-lat

TL;DR: The paper introduces and tests constrained symplectic quantization, a method that allows for the sampling of real-time quantum observables in Minkowski spacetime, providing a direct link between intrinsic time correlators and real-time Green functions. The method is applied to a 1D quantum harmonic oscillator, with numerical results agreeing with exact solutions for one- and two-point functions, energy-gap spectrum, and eigenstate probability densities.


<details>
  <summary>Details</summary>
Motivation: The motivation is to overcome the limitations of the original symplectic quantization approach, which had an ill-defined non-interacting limit and lacked a direct correspondence with Feynman path integral correlation functions. The new method, constrained symplectic quantization, aims to provide a more robust framework for simulating real-time quantum field theory dynamics that are not accessible through Euclidean simulations.

Method: Constrained symplectic quantization is introduced as a holomorphic reformulation of fields and action with constraints on the Hamiltonian flow. These constraints select stable deterministic trajectories and define convergent integration cycles for the microcanonical measure. The equivalence with the Feynman path integral at the generating functional level is established in the continuum limit. The method is then tested numerically on a 1D quantum harmonic oscillator model, with observables compared against known exact results.

Result: The application of constrained symplectic quantization to the 1D quantum harmonic oscillator yields numerical results that match well with the exact solutions for one- and two-point functions. Additionally, the characteristic oscillatory behavior of the propagator, the discrete energy-gap spectrum, and the evolution of eigenstate probability densities are successfully reconstructed, supporting the method's ability to capture real-time features of quantum systems.

Conclusion: The paper concludes that constrained symplectic quantization can effectively sample real-time quantum observables, offering a practical alternative to Euclidean-time importance sampling. It provides a clear connection between intrinsic time correlators and real-time Green functions, thus enabling the study of real-time dynamics in quantum field theories.

Abstract: Symplectic quantization is a functional approach to quantum field theory that allows sampling of quantum fluctuations directly in Minkowski space time by means of a generalized Hamiltonian dynamics in an extra time variable $τ$ which, at large times, samples a microcanonical ensemble. In a previous work we showed that, for an interacting scalar theory in 1+1 dimensions, this framework captures genuine real time features that are inaccessible to Euclidean simulations. That original formulation suffers from two structural limitations, an ill defined non interacting limit and the lack of a direct correspondence between its correlation functions and those generated by the Feynman path integral. To solve these problems we introduced constrained symplectic quantization, a holomorphic reformulation in which fields and action are analytically continued and constraints are imposed on the intrinsic time Hamiltonian flow. The constraints select stable deterministic trajectories and they define convergent holomorphic integration cycles for the corresponding microcanonical measure. In the continuum limit we establish exact equivalence with the Feynman path integral at the level of the generating functional, thus providing a direct link between intrinsic time correlators and real time Green functions. In this contribution, we apply the method to the quantum harmonic oscillator on a real-time 1-dimensional lattice. Testing various observables, we find agreement between numerical and exact results for one- and two-point functions, and we reconstruct characteristic real-time features such as an oscillatory propagator, the discrete energy-gap spectrum, and the evolution of eigenstate probability densities. These tests provide numerical evidence that constrained symplectic quantization can sample real-time quantum observables and offers a practical route beyond Euclidean-time importance sampling.

### [2] [Discretisation effects of gradient flows in QCD-like theories on the lattice](https://arxiv.org/abs/2603.05155)
*Pietro Butti,Michele Della Morte,Benjamin Jäger,Sofie Martins,J. Tobias Tsang*

Main category: hep-lat

TL;DR: The paper discusses large-scale lattice studies of the Corrigan--Ramond limit in Yang-Mills theory with a two-index antisymmetric fermion, focusing on topological properties and discretisation effects for different numbers of colors (N_C=4,5,6) and lattice spacings. The authors find that simulations might be affected by discretisation effects of around 10%.


<details>
  <summary>Details</summary>
Motivation: The motivation is to explore the Corrigan--Ramond large-$N_C$ limit of Yang-Mills theory with a two-index antisymmetric fermion, which is a step towards SUSY Yang-Mills, through large-scale lattice simulations. They aim to understand the topological properties and the impact of discretisation effects on the simulations.

Method: The researchers are generating ensembles for $N_C=4,5,6$ with lattice spacings between 0.11 - 0.08 fm. They study topological properties using a gluonic definition of the topological charge along with gradient flow, comparing Wilson and over-improved flows like DBW2. For estimating discretisation effects, they use ratios of different reference flow times.

Result: The analysis reveals that there are quantitative differences in the topological charge when using Wilson versus over-improved flows, such as DBW2. The investigation into discretisation effects suggests that the current simulations may have discretisation errors of approximately 10%.

Conclusion: The conclusion is that while the simulations provide insights into the topological properties of the system, there is a need to account for discretisation effects, which are estimated to be around 10%, when interpreting the results.

Abstract: Recent software advances now allow large-scale lattice studies of the Corrigan--Ramond large-$N_C$ limit of Yang-Mills theory coupled with a two-index antisymmetric fermion, providing a path to SUSY Yang-Mills. We are currently generating ensembles for $N_C=4,5,6$ for lattice spacings in the range $0.11 - 0.08$ fm. We report on two aspects of our work: the study of topological properties as well as estimates of discretisation effects. The first aspect is relevant since naively, fractional topological charges might be expected in our simulations. Using a gluonic definition of the topological charge combined with gradient flow, we perform an analysis of the effect of different discretisations of the kernel action, from which we identify and interpret quantitative differences between Wilson and over-improved flows such as DBW2. The second aspect is addressed by considering ratios of different reference flow times. We conclude that our current simulations might be affected by discretisation effects of order 10\%.

### [3] [Intrinsic Width of the flux tube in 2+1 dimensional Yang-Mills theories](https://arxiv.org/abs/2603.05323)
*Lorenzo Verzichelli,Michele Caselle,Elia Cellini,Alessandro Nada,Dario Panfalone*

Main category: hep-lat

TL;DR: The paper updates and analyzes the intrinsic width of the flux tube profile in (2+1)-dimensional Yang-Mills theory with an SU(2) gauge group, showing it remains constant at low temperatures but increases as the deconfinement temperature is approached.


<details>
  <summary>Details</summary>
Motivation: The motivation is to understand the behavior of the intrinsic width of the flux tube profile across a range of temperatures, particularly how it changes as the system approaches the deconfinement phase transition, which is relevant for understanding the nature of the phase transition in the context of the Svetitsky-Yaffe mapping.

Method: The authors use (2+1)-dimensional Yang-Mills theory with an SU(2) gauge group to study the flux tube. They identify the intrinsic width by examining the characteristic length scale associated with the exponentially decaying tails of the flux tube profile, and they do this over a broad range of temperatures to observe any dependency on the flux tube's length or the temperature.

Result: The results show that the intrinsic width of the flux tube profile is independent of the length of the flux tube. At low temperatures, the intrinsic width is found to be constant, while it exhibits a growing trend as the system approaches the deconfinement temperature, aligning with expectations from the universality class of the phase transition.

Conclusion: The conclusion drawn from the analysis is that the intrinsic width of the flux tube is a temperature-dependent quantity that can provide insights into the phase transition dynamics, supporting the theoretical framework provided by the Svetitsky-Yaffe conjecture.

Abstract: We present our updated results on the intrinsic width of the profile of the flux tube in (2+1)-dimensional Yang-Mills theory with SU(2) gauge group. We identify the intrinsic width as the characteristic length scale of the exponentially decaying tails of the profile of the flux tube. Inspecting a broad range of temperature, we check that this length does not depend on the length of the flux tube. Our estimations of the intrinsic width show a constant value at low temperature and a growing trend approaching the deconfinement temperature that can be understood from the universality class of the phase transition via the Svetitsky-Yaffe mapping.

</details>
