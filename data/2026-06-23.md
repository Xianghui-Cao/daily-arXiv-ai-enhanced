### [1] [Neural Wavefunctions in Quantum Field Theory I: Asymptotic Freedom](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.20791)
*Paulo F. Bedaque,Hersh Kumar,Suryansh Rajawat,Gregory Ridgway*

Main category: hep-lat

TL;DR: The paper introduces a variational approach to quantum field theory using neural network parameterized wavefunctions, overcoming traditional challenges and successfully demonstrating the method with the two-dimensional nonlinear σ-model.


<details>
  <summary>Details</summary>
Motivation: The motivation of this paper is to address the historical limitations of applying variational methods to quantum field theory by leveraging the power and flexibility of neural networks and machine learning techniques, which could potentially open up new avenues for calculations in nontrivial field theories.

Method: The authors employ a variational method where the wavefunctions are parameterized by neural networks. They use modern machine-learning techniques to optimize these wavefunctions, allowing for effective calculations in quantum field theory, particularly in scenarios that have been challenging with conventional approaches.

Result: The results show that the neural-network-based variational approach can accurately reproduce key features of the two-dimensional nonlinear σ-model, including asymptotic freedom, dynamical mass generation, and the step-scaling function, indicating the potential of the method for tackling complex field theories.

Conclusion: The conclusion of the paper is that the combination of variational methods and neural network parameterized wavefunctions, along with advanced machine-learning, provides a powerful tool for performing competitive calculations in nontrivial quantum field theories, as demonstrated by the successful application to the 2D nonlinear σ-model.

Abstract: We present a variational approach to quantum field theory based on wavefunctions parameterized by neural networks. While variational methods have a celebrated history across many fields, their application to quantum field theory has been limited by well-known challenges. We show that neural-network wavefunctions, combined with modern machine-learning techniques, enable competitive variational calculations in nontrivial field theories. As a demonstration, we reproduce the essential features of the two-dimensional nonlinear $σ$-model: asymptotic freedom, dynamical mass generation and the model's step-scaling function.

*Benoît Blossier,Manon Michel,Yacob Ozdalkiran*

Main category: hep-lat

TL;DR: The paper introduces two implementations of the Event-Chain Monte Carlo (ECMC) algorithm for Yang-Mills SU(N) lattice gauge theories, demonstrating their validity through numerical tests that show agreement with conventional Monte Carlo methods.


<details>
  <summary>Details</summary>
Motivation: The motivation is to develop and validate a new sampling scheme, specifically the ECMC algorithm, for Yang-Mills lattice gauge theories which could potentially offer more efficient or accurate simulations compared to conventional Monte Carlo methods.

Method: The method involves creating two implementations of the ECMC algorithm designed for general SU(N) Yang-Mills theories with the Wilson action, and then testing these on four-dimensional lattices for the case N=3. The algorithms feature local ballistic updates and stochastic events, forming an irreversible and rejection-free Markov process that satisfies global balance.

Result: The results from the numerical tests on 4D lattices indicate that standard gauge observables, such as the mean plaquette, are in agreement with those obtained using traditional Monte Carlo techniques, thereby validating the ECMC approach for this context.

Conclusion: The conclusion is that the ECMC algorithm can be a viable alternative for sampling in Yang-Mills lattice gauge theories, as it has been shown to reproduce known results for gauge observables, suggesting its potential for future applications in the field.

Abstract: We develop two implementations of the Event-Chain Monte Carlo (ECMC) algorithm for Yang-Mills $\mathrm{SU}(N)$ lattice gauge theories with the Wilson action. These algorithms consist in a succession of local ballistic updates intersped with stochastic events, resulting in an irreversible and rejection-free Markov process. The resulting dynamics satisfy global balance, ensuring the correct equilibrium distribution. The algorithms are formulated for general $\mathrm{SU}(N)$ Yang-Mills theories with Wilson action and implemented for the case $N=3$. Numerical tests on four-dimensional lattices show that standard gauge observables, such as the mean plaquette, agree with results obtained using conventional Monte Carlo algorithms. These results provide a first validation of ECMC as a viable sampling scheme for Yang-Mills lattice gauge theories.

TL;DR: This paper presents a semi-simple cubic (ssc) lattice approach for quantum computing in lattice gauge theories, which is more efficient in qubit usage and better at handling Gauss's law. The authors aim to reduce the number of qubits needed and improve the handling of Gauss's law.

Method: The method involves creating a semi-simple cubic (ssc) lattice by removing half of the gauge links from a standard cubic lattice, ensuring every vertex is trivalent. This lattice is designed to host fermions and non-Abelian gauge fields, specifically staggered fermions with SU(2) gauge fields, while being more efficient in terms of qubit requirements.

Result: The result is a new lattice structure, the ssc lattice, which is topologically equivalent to the triamond lattice but with the advantage of having gauge links at each vertex spanning all three directions, allowing for a local fermion derivative. This makes the ssc lattice a promising platform for quantum simulations of gauge theories on near-term quantum hardware.

Conclusion: The conclusion of the paper suggests that the semi-simple cubic lattice offers a viable and more efficient alternative to the simple cubic lattice for simulating lattice gauge theories on quantum computers, particularly for incorporating fermions and non-Abelian gauge fields.

Abstract: A practical Hamiltonian approach to lattice gauge theories would provide access to several important areas of phenomenology that have been beyond the reach of conventional lattice methods. Quantum computers seem to be a natural platform for this approach. Specifically, the semi-simple cubic (ssc) lattice is obtained by removing half of the gauge links from a standard cubic lattice in such a way that every vertex becomes trivalent, which streamlines the handling of Gauss's law. The ssc lattice is topologically equivalent to the triamond lattice but, because the gauge links at each vertex span all three directions, the ssc lattice can accommodate a local fermion derivative. The case of staggered fermions with SU(2) gauge fields is presented here.

### [5] [Multi-particle states investigation with tensor renormalization group method](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.22321)
*Fathiyya Izzatun Az-zahra,Shinji Takeda,Takeshi Yamazaki*

Main category: hep-lat

TL;DR: Researchers used a spectroscopy scheme, combining transfer matrix and tensor renormalization group methods, to investigate multi-particle states in the (1+1)d Ising Model. They identified quantum numbers and momenta of energy eigenstates, determined particle numbers by system size dependency, and calculated scattering phase shifts for two-particle states, which were consistent with theoretical predictions. This data was further utilized to study the degeneracy of two- and three-particle states and predict three-particle finite volume energy.

Motivation: The motivation is to understand the properties of multi-particle states within the (1+1)d Ising Model, including their quantum numbers, momenta, and particle interactions such as scattering phase shifts and state degeneracies.

Method: The method involves a spectroscopy scheme that uses a transfer matrix estimated by a coarse-grained tensor network to compute the energy spectrum. An interpolating operator, computed via an impurity tensor network, is used to identify the quantum numbers and momenta. The number of particles in the states is inferred from the system's energy dependence on its size. Scattering phase shifts are calculated using Lüscher's formula and wave function approach, and these are used to examine the degeneracy of multi-particle states and predict three-particle finite volume energy.

Result: The results include the identification of one-, two-, and three-particle states, computation of two-particle scattering phase shifts that match theoretical predictions, and an investigation into the degeneracy of two- and three-particle states along with the prediction of three-particle finite volume energy.

Conclusion: The study successfully applies a combination of transfer matrix and tensor renormalization group methods to characterize multi-particle states in the (1+1)d Ising Model, providing insights into their scattering behavior and state degeneracies.

Abstract: We investigate multi-particle states of the (1+1)d Ising Model using a spectroscopy scheme based on transfer matrix and tensor renormalization group method. The scheme begins with computing the energy spectrum of the system from the transfer matrix estimated by the coarse-grained tensor network. The quantum number and momentum of these energy eigenstates are not a priori known, thus we identify them using matrix elements of an interpolating operator that is numerically computed with an impurity tensor network. Furthermore, by observing the dependence of the energy as a function of system size, we identify the number of particles of the eigenstates and obtain one-, two-, and three-particle states for a specific quantum number and momentum. From the two-particle state sector, we compute the scattering phase shift using Lüscher's formula and wave function approach, and observe their consistency with theoretical prediction. Using the information of the two-particle scattering phase shift, we investigate the degeneracy of the two-particle states, the theoretical prediction of the three-particle finite volume energy and also the degeneracy in the three-particle states.
