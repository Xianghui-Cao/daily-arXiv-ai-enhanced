Abstract: Relativistic wavefunctions of nucleon excitations are scrutinised to understand their node structure and the underlying role of local interpolating fields in generating the nucleon spectrum. In addressing quark model perspectives, approximately 4000 propagators are employed on the heaviest PACS-CS ensemble at $m_π\simeq$ 702 MeV. We examine the ground and four lowest-lying excited states at zero momentum for both positive- and negative-parity spectra, where the proton's d-quark wavefunction is calculated about the two u quarks at the origin. This is achieved using two local interpolating fields that each carry the quantum numbers of the nucleon but with differing spin-flavour structures, one of which vanishes in the nonrelativistic limit. We find that two distinct types of wavefunction nodes are manifest: "superposition nodes" formed through a linear combination of interpolating fields, and novel "built-in nodes" that are fundamentally built in to the s-wave Dirac components of an individual interpolating field. These are investigated qualitatively through visualisations in the form of both volume and surface renderings, and quantitatively by the calculation of radial wavefunctions. Combined, these findings build a comprehensive picture of the single-particle nucleon spectrum and how its properties derive from fundamental lattice operators.

### [2] [Scalable Generative Sampling and Multilevel Estimation for Lattice Field Theories Near Criticality](https://arxiv.org/abs/2604.10209)
*A. Singha,J. Kauffmann,E. Cellini,K. Jansen,S. Nakajima*

Main category: hep-lat

TL;DR: A multiscale generative sampler, which is inspired by renormalization-group ideas and uses a coarse-to-fine hierarchy to model the Boltzmann distribution, significantly reduces critical slowing down in sampling lattice field theories near criticality. It achieves much smaller integrated autocorrelation times than Hybrid Monte Carlo (HMC) for 2D scalar φ^4 theory at criticality on large volumes, while maintaining high importance-sampling efficiency and accurately reproducing physical observables.

Motivation: The motivation of this paper is to address the issue of critical slowing down, which hinders efficient sampling of lattice field theories at or near critical points, especially as the lattice volume increases. Traditional Markov chain methods become less effective in such scenarios, and thus there is a need for more efficient sampling techniques that can handle the computational challenges associated with large lattices and strong correlations between degrees of freedom.

Method: The method introduced in the paper is a multiscale generative sampler that is based on renormalization-group concepts. It constructs a hierarchical model of the Boltzmann distribution using a sequence of conditional Gaussian mixture models and masked continuous normalizing flows. The process starts with a coarse representation of the field and progressively adds finer details. This approach allows the sampler to capture long-wavelength modes at coarser levels and short-distance fluctuations at finer levels, while also preserving exact restriction maps for unbiased Multilevel Monte Carlo variance reduction.

Result: The results show that the proposed multiscale generative sampler has integrated autocorrelation times that are orders of magnitude smaller than those obtained with Hybrid Monte Carlo (HMC) when applied to the two-dimensional scalar φ^4 theory at criticality, particularly on large lattices. The method also maintains a high level of importance-sampling efficiency and is able to reproduce unbiased physical observables that are in statistical agreement with those from long HMC simulations.

Conclusion: The conclusion of the paper is that the multiscale generative sampler effectively mitigates the problem of critical slowing down in lattice field theories, providing a more efficient alternative to traditional Markov chain methods like HMC. By leveraging a coarse-to-fine hierarchy and renormalization-group ideas, it not only reduces autocorrelation times but also ensures accurate and efficient sampling of physical observables, making it a promising tool for simulating large-scale lattice systems near critical points.

Abstract: Sampling lattice field theories near criticality is severely hindered by critical slowing down, which makes standard Markov chain methods increasingly inefficient at large lattice volumes. We introduce a multiscale generative sampler, inspired by renormalization-group ideas, that models the Boltzmann distribution through a coarse-to-fine hierarchy across length scales. At each level, a conditional Gaussian mixture model captures the main local dependence of newly introduced variables on the already-sampled coarse field, while a masked continuous normalizing flow refines the remaining conditional structure. Coarse levels encode the dominant long-wavelength modes, and finer levels progressively add short-distance fluctuations. In addition, because the architecture preserves coarse fields exactly during refinement, it provides exact restriction maps at no additional computational cost and directly enables unbiased Multilevel Monte Carlo (MLMC) variance reduction. For the two-dimensional scalar $φ^4$ theory at criticality, the method achieves integrated autocorrelation times orders of magnitude smaller than Hybrid Monte Carlo (HMC) on large volumes, maintains high importance-sampling efficiency relative to other generative baselines, and reproduces unbiased physical observables in statistical agreement with long HMC simulations.
