### [1] [Wilson loops with oppositely oriented plaquettes as a probe of center vortex structure](https://arxiv.org/abs/2603.13757)
*Ji-Chong Yang,Xiang-Ning Li,Zhan Zhao*

Main category: hep-lat

TL;DR: The study explores Wilson loops with opposite orientations in lattice gauge theory, observing that while the vertical configuration aligns with vortex picture expectations, the parallel configuration shows an unexpected deviation. A new qualitative vortex model is introduced to explain this behavior, maintaining consistency with the vortex framework.


<details>
  <summary>Details</summary>
Motivation: The motivation of the paper is to investigate Wilson loops with nontrivial orientation structures in the context of lattice gauge theory as a means to probe and validate the center vortex picture. The researchers aim to understand how the orientation of plaquettes within these loops affects their behavior and whether such behaviors conform to or deviate from the predictions of the vortex picture, particularly focusing on configurations with opposite orientations.

Method: The method involves constructing and analyzing Wilson loops that contain two plaquettes with opposite orientations. These are studied in two specific geometrical setups: the 'vertical' and 'parallel' configurations. Through these setups, the researchers assess the conformity of the observed loop behaviors with the expected outcomes from the center vortex picture, and develop a qualitative vortex model to account for any deviations found, especially in the parallel configuration.

Result: The results show that the vertical configuration of the Wilson loops behaves as expected according to the vortex picture. However, the parallel configuration exhibits a deviation from the simple area-law expectation, which cannot be fully explained by the opposite orientations alone. This deviation is addressed through the introduction of a novel, qualitative vortex model that successfully accounts for the observed phenomena, thereby supporting the vortex picture's validity even in more complex scenarios.

Conclusion: The conclusion drawn from the study is that, despite the initial deviation observed in the parallel configuration, the overall findings support the applicability and explanatory power of the center vortex picture within the scope of lattice gauge theory. The newly proposed vortex model provides a coherent explanation for the observed behavior, suggesting that the vortex framework remains robust and adaptable to accommodate more intricate loop structures and their dynamics.

Abstract: We study Wilson loops with a nontrivial orientation structure in lattice gauge theory as a probe of the center vortex picture. The observable is a single Wilson loop containing two plaquettes with opposite orientations, realized in two geometries referred to as the vertical and parallel configurations. The vertical case behaves consistently with expectations from the vortex picture. In contrast, the parallel configuration shows a deviation from the naive area-law expectation which cannot be explained solely by the opposite orientations of the plaquettes. We introduce a simple qualitative vortex model which accounts for this behavior and shows that the observed effect can still be understood within the vortex framework.

### [2] [Electric Polarizability of Charged Pions from nHYP Four-Point Functions](https://arxiv.org/abs/2603.15231)
*Benjamin Luke,Sudip Shiwakoti,Shayan Nadeem,Andrei Alexandru,Walter Wilcox,Frank X. Lee*

Main category: hep-lat

TL;DR: The paper presents an improved method for calculating the electric polarizability of charged pions using four-point functions, with enhancements like a dynamical action, smaller pion masses, and variable lattice sizes to better extrapolate to infinite volume.


<details>
  <summary>Details</summary>
Motivation: The motivation is to improve upon the traditional external field two-point function method by using four-point functions for a more effective calculation of polarizabilities in both charged and neutral hadrons, particularly focusing on the electric polarizability of the charged pion with a more realistic quark dynamics and at lower pion masses.

Method: The authors use a dynamical nHYP action on a lattice, with pion masses of 220 MeV and 315 MeV, and vary the lattice size to allow for extrapolation to infinite volume, as opposed to their previous work which used a quenched Wilson action with higher pion masses.

Result: Preliminary results are provided, indicating the progress made with the new methodology that includes a dynamical action and more suitable pion masses, although specific numerical values or detailed findings are not given in the abstract.

Conclusion: The study suggests that the improvements in the methodology, such as the use of a dynamical action and the reduction in pion mass, will lead to a more accurate determination of the electric polarizability of the charged pion, and set the stage for further research in this area.

Abstract: Understanding a hadron's electric and magnetic polarizabilities allows one to access internal structural information. Traditionally, the external field two-point function method has been used to calculate polarizabilities. However, recent work has demonstrated the effectiveness of using four-point functions for computing polarizabilities of charged and neutral hadrons. Our previous study on the electric polarizability of the charged pion used a quenched Wilson action on a lattice with pion mass from 1100 MeV to 370 MeV. In this work, we employ a number of improvements, including a dynamical action (nHYP), smaller pion masses (220 MeV and 315 MeV), and a variable lattice size in order to extrapolate to infinite volume. Preliminary results are presented.

### [3] [Standard Model tests with smeared experiment and theory](https://arxiv.org/abs/2603.15487)
*Andreas Jüttner*

Main category: hep-lat

TL;DR: The paper proposes using spectral-reconstruction techniques for Standard Model processes, enabling model-independent lattice QCD predictions. It suggests performing Standard Model tests with both experimental and theoretical results being energy-smeared, as a way to overcome the challenge of achieving sufficiently small smearing in lattice simulations.


<details>
  <summary>Details</summary>
Motivation: The motivation is to provide a method for making model-independent predictions in lattice QCD, particularly for processes involving on-shell intermediate hadronic states, by employing spectral-reconstruction techniques. The paper aims to address the challenge of achieving a controlled extrapolation to vanishing smearing width in lattice simulations, which is currently difficult with existing methods.

Method: The method involves the use of spectral-reconstruction techniques to compute the energy-smeared spectral density, leading to the physical amplitude as the smearing width approaches zero. The paper also introduces the idea of conducting Standard Model tests where both experimental data and theory predictions are smeared with a finite width, similar to the approach used in the analysis of the muon (g-2).

Result: The result is a proposal for a new approach to compare lattice QCD predictions with experimental data, which does not require an extrapolation to zero smearing width. This is illustrated through concrete examples such as inclusive meson decay and long-distance contributions to rare semileptonic meson decays.

Conclusion: The conclusion is that by applying a consistent smearing to both experimental and theoretical sides, it is possible to perform meaningful comparisons and tests of the Standard Model, even when the lattice QCD calculations have a finite smearing width.

Abstract: For Standard Model processes in which on-shell intermediate hadronic states contribute - including inclusive semileptonic decays and long-distance effects in rare exclusive decays such as $D\to π\ell\ell$ and $B\to K^{(\ast)}\ell\ell$ - spectral-reconstruction techniques provide a promising route to model-independent lattice QCD predictions for use in phenomenological predictions. The central ingredient is the computation of the energy-smeared spectral density. Following the continuum and infinite-volume limits, the physical amplitude is recovered as the limit of vanishing smearing width. However, achieving sufficiently small smearing for a controlled extrapolation remains a significant challenge for current lattice simulations. In this paper, we therefore propose Standard Model tests, in which both experimental results and theory predictions are smeared with finite width, similar to what has previously been done in the literature for experimental and lattice $R$-ratio data in the context of the muon $(g-2)_μ$. As concrete examples, we discuss the cases of inclusive meson decay and long-distance contributions to rare semileptonic meson decay.

</details>
