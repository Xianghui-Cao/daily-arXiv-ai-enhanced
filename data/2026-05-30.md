Main category: hep-lat

TL;DR: A 2dVoid model is developed to identify center vortices in 2D SU(2) lattice configurations, with a training set created by inserting and distorting Z2 vortices. The model shows reliability in vortex location identification and scalability for higher dimensions.

  <summary>Details</summary>
Motivation: The motivation behind this paper is to take the initial steps towards enabling machines to identify confining objects, specifically center vortices, within thermalized lattice gauge theory configurations. This could potentially lead to more efficient and automated analysis of such systems, which are fundamental to understanding the strong force in quantum chromodynamics (QCD).

Method: The authors developed a 2dVoid model designed for identifying center vortices on pure SU(2) lattices in two dimensions. They generated a training dataset by introducing thin Z2 vortices into zero action lattices and then applying distortions through random SU(2) gauge transformations, noise addition, and cooling to thicken the vortices. Result: For vortices that have moderate visibility, the 2dVoid model demonstrated the ability to accurately pinpoint the location of center vortices. Additionally, the researchers showed that their approach can be scaled up to work in higher dimensions by employing tiling strategies, which also has the benefit of reducing the costs associated with training the model.

Conclusion: The 2dVoid model presented in the paper successfully identifies the location of center vortices in 2D SU(2) lattices, even after the application of various distortions. The scalability of the method through tiling suggests potential for its use in more complex, higher-dimensional lattice configurations, thereby advancing the field of automated detection of confining objects in lattice gauge theories.

Abstract: As a first step towards machine identification of confining objects in thermalized lattice gauge configurations, we present our 2dVoId model for center vortex identification on pure SU(2) lattices in $D = 2$ dimensions. We create a training set by inserting thin Z2 vortices at various locations on a zero action lattice, and then distort those configurations by applying random SU(2) gauge transformations, noise, and by thickening the vortices via cooling. For moderate vortex visibility, our model is able to reliably identify the location of center vortices. We additionally demonstrate scalability through tiling strategies, which will enable generalization to higher dimensions while reducing training costs.

Method: The method involves performing a lattice QCD calculation using CLS ensembles, examining finite-volume effects, taking the continuum limit, and extrapolating to the physical point for several values of the UV cutoff scale Λ. The Cottingham formula is used to decompose the results into elastic and inelastic contributions at fixed Λ. The final step includes removing the cutoff scale Λ from the result.

Result: The final result, after the removal of the cutoff scale Λ, is a charged-neutral pion mass splitting of M_{π^+} - M_{π^0} = 4.56(22) MeV, which is in good agreement with the experimental measurement.

Abstract: We present a lattice QCD calculation of the charged-neutral pion mass splitting $M_{π^+} - M_{π^0}$ at $\mathcal{O}(α_\mathrm{em})$ using a recently proposed framework based on a Pauli-Villars (PV) regulated photon propagator defined in the continuum and infinite-volume limit, with $Λ$ acting as an additional UV cutoff scale. The use of this propagator avoids power-law finite-volume effects, allowing for a straightforward treatment of the infinite-volume limit. By means of the Cottingham formula, we further decompose the result into elastic and inelastic contributions at fixed $Λ$. Our final result, after removing the cutoff scale $Λ$, is $M_{π^+} - M_{π^0} = 4.56(22)$ MeV, in good agreement with the experimental measurement. This calculation serves as a validation of the formalism in a well-controlled setting and offers useful insights into the application of electromagnetic corrections to other observables.

### [6] [Mellin Moments of the Unpolarized Gluon PDF in the Proton from Nonlocal Operators in Lattice QCD](https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.30193)
*Joseph Delmar,Krzysztof Cichy,Martha Constantinou,Yong Zhao*

Main category: hep-lat

TL;DR: The paper presents a lattice QCD study to determine the Mellin moments of the unpolarized gluon parton distribution function in the proton, using nonlocal gluon operators and boosted proton states. The analysis is conducted with a specific fermion ensemble and gauge action, and the results include an extraction of the ratio $\langle x^3\rangle_g/\langle x\rangle_g$ at 2 GeV, with uncertainties that encompass statistical and theoretical systematics.


<details>

Motivation: The motivation for this research is to provide a precise determination of the gluon parton distribution function within the proton, which is essential for understanding the internal structure of protons and for making accurate predictions in high-energy physics experiments.

Method: The method involves using lattice Quantum Chromodynamics (QCD) with a particular fermion ensemble and gauge action. The researchers calculate matrix elements of nonlocal gluon operators coupled to momentum-boosted proton states and use the operator product expansion (OPE) to extract higher-order gluon moments relative to the gluon momentum fraction. They also investigate systematic effects and apply DGLAP equations to study scale evolution.

Result: The result is the determination of the ratio $\langle x^3\rangle_g/\langle x\rangle_g$ at a scale of 2 GeV, with comprehensive uncertainty estimates that include both statistical and the main theoretical systematic uncertainties. The stability of the extracted moments is examined through scale evolution.

Conclusion: The conclusion drawn from this work is that it provides a robust determination of the gluon parton distribution function's Mellin moments, which contributes to the knowledge of the proton's internal structure and has implications for high-energy physics. The study also highlights the importance of considering systematic effects and the role of scale evolution in the analysis.

Abstract: We present a lattice QCD determination of the Mellin moments of the unpolarized gluon parton distribution function in the proton. The analysis is based on matrix elements of nonlocal gluon operators coupled to momentum-boosted proton states. The calculation relies on an $N_f=2+1+1$ ensemble of maximally twisted mass fermions with clover improvement and the Iwasaki-improved gauge action, at a pion mass of approximately 260 MeV. Working within the short-distance operator product expansion (OPE) of the reduced gluon Ioffe-time distribution, we extract ratios of higher-order gluon moments, $\langle x^n\rangle$ with $n>1$, to the gluon momentum fraction, $\langle x\rangle$. We investigate systematic effects associated with the truncation of the order of moment in the OPE, the choice of minimum and maximum Wilson-line separations entering the analysis, and the treatment of mixing with the quark-singlet under perturbative matching. The stability of the extracted moments is further studied under scale evolution using DGLAP equations, allowing us to assess uncertainties related to perturbative truncation by varying the scale. Our work provides a determination of the ratio $\langle x^3\rangle_g/\langle x\rangle_g$ at a scale of 2 GeV, with uncertainties that account for both statistical and the dominant theoretical systematic uncertainties.
