### [1] [Tensor renormalization group approach to the $O(2)$ models via symmetry-twisted partition functions](https://arxiv.org/abs/2603.02739)
*Shinichiro Akiyama,Raghav G. Jha,Jun Maeda,Yuya Tanizaki,Judah Unmuth-Yockey*

Main category: hep-lat

TL;DR: The paper investigates critical phenomena in O(2) models using symmetry-twisted partition functions, which are computed with the tensor renormalization group. It demonstrates the detection of spontaneous symmetry breaking and determines the BKT transition point in 2D, as well as identifies phase transitions in a generalized 2D O(2) model.


<details>
  <summary>Details</summary>
Motivation: The motivation is to explore and confirm the effectiveness of symmetry-twisted partition functions in identifying critical phenomena such as spontaneous symmetry breaking and phase transitions in O(2) models, particularly at the BKT transition and between different phases in two dimensions.

Method: The method involves the use of symmetry-twisted partition functions within the tensor renormalization group framework to compute and analyze the properties of O(2) models in both three and two dimensions, including the helicity modulus for determining the BKT transition point.

Result: The results show that the symmetry-twisted partition functions can detect spontaneous breaking of global continuous symmetry and provide the helicity modulus for the BKT transition. Additionally, the approach successfully identifies phase transitions in a 2D generalized O(2) model.

Conclusion: The conclusion is that the symmetry-twisted partition function approach is effective in detecting spontaneous symmetry breaking, determining the BKT transition, and identifying various phase transitions in O(2) models, thus confirming its utility in the study of critical phenomena.

Abstract: We investigate critical phenomena in the $O(2)$ models using symmetry-twisted partition functions that can be efficiently computed within the tensor renormalization group framework. We first demonstrate, taking the three-dimensional model as an example, that symmetry-twisted partition functions detect the spontaneous breaking of global continuous symmetry. We then consider the same model in two dimensions, where the Berezinskii--Kosterlitz--Thouless (BKT) transition occurs. Since symmetry-twisted partition functions directly provide the helicity modulus at a finite twist angle, we determine the BKT transition point. These results are presented based on Ref.~\cite{Akiyama:2026dzg}. Finally, in addition to the original paper~\cite{Akiyama:2026dzg}, we apply this approach to the two-dimensional generalized $O(2)$ model and confirm that it successfully identifies the phase transitions between the ferromagnetic and nematic phases, as well as between the nematic and paramagnetic phases.

Main category: hep-lat

TL;DR: This paper demonstrates the use of normalizing flows to create unbiased, reduced-variance estimators for lattice field theory observables, showing significant variance reduction in glueball correlation functions and gluonic matrix elements, with benefits that scale well with lattice volume.


<details>
  <summary>Details</summary>
Motivation: The motivation is to improve the accuracy and efficiency of simulations in lattice field theories, particularly for observables defined by derivatives with respect to action parameters, by reducing the variance in the estimators used.

Method: The method involves implementing normalizing flows for observables that include gluonic operator insertions within SU(3) Yang-Mills theory and two-flavor QCD. This approach aims at achieving a substantial variance reduction which can be leveraged across different lattice volumes.

Result: Variance reduction by factors of 10-60 was achieved in specific cases, such as glueball correlation functions and gluonic matrix elements relevant to hadron structure. The computational advantages were shown, and the variance reduction was found to be roughly independent of the lattice volume.

Conclusion: The study concludes that normalizing flows provide a computationally advantageous method for reducing variance in lattice field theory simulations, with potential for significant cost savings through volume transfer, minimizing training costs.

Abstract: Normalizing flows can be used to construct unbiased, reduced-variance estimators for lattice field theory observables that are defined by a derivative with respect to action parameters. This work implements the approach for observables involving gluonic operator insertions in the SU(3) Yang-Mills theory and two-flavor Quantum Chromodynamics (QCD) in four space-time dimensions. Variance reduction by factors of $10$-$60$ is achieved in glueball correlation functions and in gluonic matrix elements related to hadron structure, with demonstrated computational advantages. The observed variance reduction is found to be approximately independent of the lattice volume, so that volume transfer can be utilized to minimize training costs.

Main category: hep-lat

TL;DR: A new generative architecture using physics-informed kernels (PIKs) is introduced to address complex probability distribution sampling, tackling sign problems and enabling efficient sampling. Its effectiveness is shown in zero-dimensional field theories and real-time quantum harmonic oscillator evolution.

  <summary>Details</summary>
Motivation: The motivation for this paper is to develop a method that can effectively sample from systems with complex probability distributions, while addressing the common issues of sign problems and inefficient sampling.

Method: The researchers developed a novel generative architecture based on physics-informed kernels (PIKs). This architecture leverages the probability-weight preserving property of PIKs to map the sampling task onto a sign-problem free manifold where sampling is more straightforward and efficient.

Result: The results demonstrate the potential of the PIK-architecture through its application to two specific scenarios: zero-dimensional field theories with complex couplings and the real-time evolution of the quantum-mechanical harmonic oscillator. The method shows promise in resolving both the sign problem and achieving efficient sampling.

Conclusion: The proposed PIK-architecture successfully addresses the challenges of sign problems and efficient sampling in systems with complex probability distributions, as evidenced by its successful application to theoretical and quantum mechanical models.

Abstract: In the present work we construct a novel generative architecture for systems with complex probability distributions. In general, these sampling tasks come with two challenges: resolving sign problems and efficient sampling. The architecture is based on physics-informed kernels (PIKs) introduced in arXiv:2510.26678, and aims at resolving both challenges. Key to the complex PIK-architecture is its probability-weight preserving property, which allows us to map the sampling task to one on a sign-problem free manifold with a simple distribution and efficient sampling. The potential of this novel architecture is demonstrated within applications to zero-dimensional field theories with complex couplings, as well as the real-time evolution of the quantum-mechanical harmonic oscillator.
